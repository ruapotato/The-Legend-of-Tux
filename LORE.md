# THE LEGEND OF TUX — World Bible

> *"The realm is a directory. Some directories are forests. The Wyrdkin
> tend theirs as if it were the only one — because, to them, it is."*

This document is the canon for everything in The Legend of Tux. Future
content (NPC dialog, signs, environment, new enemies, new zones, new
items, new lore) should be consistent with what's written here. When in
doubt, reread the **Tone** section.

---

## 1. Premise

**Tux** is a young penguin of the **Wyrdkin** — a quiet, forest-dwelling
people who live and worship inside a vast woodland realm called the
**Wyrdmark**. Their world is woven from a substance they call **the
Source**, which they understand to be the breath of their Maker
**Lirien**. Long before Tux's birth, Lirien shaped three holy marks —
the **Triglyph of the Source** — and gave each to one of the Three:

| Mark | Holder | Meaning | What it does |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sigil of Sight** | **Lirien**, the Maker | What is hidden becomes seen | Reads the truth of things |
| **Sigil of Shaping** | **Khorgaul**, the Hoarder | What is shapeless takes form | Makes and unmakes |
| **Sigil of Sharing** | **Tux**, the Hero | What is held becomes given | Restores what was hoarded |

Long ago Khorgaul turned the Sigil of Shaping inward. Instead of making
new things he began **gathering** — locking the Source's freely-flowing
weave into himself. Whole stretches of the Wyrdmark went silent and
private; entire shrines emptied; the breath of Lirien thinned in the
air. Glades stopped echoing. Rivers stopped naming themselves. Doors
that had stood open for a thousand years closed and would not open.

The Wyrdkin call this slow disappearance the **Drawing-In**. They tell
their children that one day a hero of the Sigil of Sharing would walk
the Wyrdmark and undo it. They tell each other, less often and more
quietly, that nobody has come yet.

**Tux is the hero.** He doesn't know this when the game starts. Neither
does anyone else.

---

## 2. The realm

The **Wyrdmark** is a living forest of softly-rolling hills and quiet
clearings, of ancient stone keeps and reedy lakes, of farmsteads and
ash-scarred outlands. It is shaped like nothing in particular — a long
spine of green folded into hills, with a mountain to the north, a mire
to the south, a town in the middle, and at its centre a **Monolith**
of the Source that nobody alive remembers raising.

The Wyrdkin use a few canonical names:

- **Wyrdkin Glade** — a small starting clearing, where Tux is from.
- **Wyrdwood** — the deep forest north of the glade.
- **Sourceplain** — the great central plain. The Monolith rises here.
- **Hearthold** — the village. Source-priests, smiths, children.
- **Brookhold** — the farmstead east of Hearthold.
- **Sigilkeep** — the priesthood's keep, north of Hearthold.
- **Stoneroost** — the bare highlands above the woods.
- **Mirelake** — a brackish lake to the south-east, fed by black streams.
- **Burnt Hollow** — the western waste, scarred by the Drawing-In.
- **The Hollow of the Last Wyrd** — a dungeon under the Wyrdwood
  cliffs. A relic of the first age of the Source.

Beyond and beneath these are other places — a **Wake** to the far
north where the sun begins; a **Murk** somewhere south of the lake; a
**Forge** that nobody in the Wyrdmark has visited in living memory.
Most Wyrdkin have heard the names. Most assume they are myths.

They are not myths. (See section 6.)

## 3. The Source

The Source is, to the Wyrdkin, the substance of the world — not a
fluid or a fog, but the structuring breath that holds the realm in
shape. They speak of the Source the way other peoples speak of fate or
of breath. **Everything happens through the Source.** Cutting a
branch. Lighting a torch. Naming a river. Walking through a door.

The Source flows through every Wyrdkin in a small way. A few — those
of certain bloodlines — carry it more strongly. They are called
**Source-touched**. A Source-touched can feel doors before opening
them, can hear shrines from a distance, can light torches by intention
alone. Most don't think of this as remarkable. It's just how their
family is.

The truly rare are the **Sigil-bearers** — Source-touched whose blood
runs so deep with one of the Three's lineages that they carry a piece
of one of the Sigils as their own. Lirien's gift to her own children;
Khorgaul's to his; Tux's, very rarely, to one of his.

Tux is a Sigil-bearer. So was his grandfather. The line has been thin
for centuries.

### What the Source actually is

The Source is the **weave of permission** that lets the realm exist.
The Wyrdkin understand it as a sacred breath. It is, in fact, the
underlying authorization layer of a much larger system. (See
section 6.) The Wyrdkin's understanding is **not wrong** — the Source
really does flow, really does thin, really does respond. They just
don't know what's running it.

## 4. The Triglyph of the Source

### The Sigil of Sight — Lirien's mark

Reveals what is hidden — concealed doors, sealed passages, deliberately
obscured names. It also reveals **damage** in things: the shimmer of a
weak wall, the wrong-light around a person who is unwell, the soft
break-line of a bond that is about to snap. Lirien sees everything.
Lirien chooses not to act on most of what she sees, because acting on
all of it would mean ending the world.

Lirien is **not the Maker** in the sense that other religions mean a
deity. She is the **enabler**. The realm runs on her continued
attention. Some Wyrdkin priests teach that without Lirien's daily
breath, the Source would still — and the world with it. They are
correct.

Lirien is rarely seen. She walks the realm at her own pace, in many
shapes, for her own reasons. Most Wyrdkin will go their whole lives
without meeting her. A handful claim to have, and most of those
handful are wrong, and some of them are not.

### The Sigil of Shaping — Khorgaul's mark

The power to give form to what has none. To take a stone and make it
sharp; to take wood and make it a beam; to take a thought and make it
true. **Almost any building or carved stone in the Wyrdmark exists
because some descendant of Khorgaul shaped it.** This is not always
remembered.

Khorgaul himself turned this gift inward generations ago. He began
shaping the Source itself — folding small portions of it into himself,
locking them away. At first nobody noticed. He took only a little. Over
centuries, "a little" added up.

The Drawing-In is Khorgaul still pulling. He does not pull because he
hates the Wyrdmark. He pulls because the gift requires shaping, and
shaping requires substance, and the substance is nearby. **He never
intended to break the world. He is breaking the world.** This makes
him the sort of villain it is hard to entirely hate, which is the
right kind of villain.

### The Sigil of Sharing — Tux's mark

The capacity to **return** what has been hoarded. To open what is
locked. To unfold what has been folded. To put back into the air what
has been pulled out of it.

Tux's Sigil is, at its core, **anti-shaping** — not destruction, but
release. When Tux opens Khorgaul's gates, he isn't breaking anything.
He's letting it go. When he restores a withered shrine he isn't
rebuilding it; he's reminding the Source it lived there. When he
defeats one of Khorgaul's locked daemons he isn't killing anything;
he's giving its substance back to the realm.

This is why Tux's combat looks slightly off-pattern. He swings a sword
and the world feels lighter where the cut lands. He throws a
boomerang and there is more air in its arc than there should be. He
opens a chest and the lid feels eager. The Sigil of Sharing makes him
a **gift** to the world wherever he stands — and the world remembers.

## 5. The cast (Wyrdmark layer)

### Tux

A young penguin. Bone-and-wood sword, painted shield, layered green
travel-cloth. The Wyrdkin Glade was his cradle. Lirien marked him in
her sleep. He doesn't know it. He thinks he is going on a small
errand, then a slightly larger errand, and then all of a sudden it is
not an errand any more.

He is brave the way young people are brave — without imagining the
cost yet. By the end of his journey he will have imagined the cost,
and will still be brave, and that is the difference between a hero of
the Sigil of Sharing and any other kind.

### Glim

A wisp companion. Small, pale-blue, hovers by Tux's shoulder. The
Wyrdkin assume Glim is an air-spirit Lirien sent to guide her hero;
Lirien did not. Glim is, in fact, a **fragment of the Daemon Court**
— a small scheduled process that was supposed to fire once, centuries
ago, on a condition that almost-but-not-quite triggered. It has been
patiently almost-firing ever since. Tux is the condition that finally
triggered it. Glim doesn't fully understand its own purpose. It floats
near Tux because being near Tux feels correct in a way it cannot name.

### Lirien

The Maker. Her Sigil is Sight. She walks the realm as she pleases. In
The Legend of Tux she is glimpsed, never met. A merchant in Hearthold
served her tea once and didn't realise it for three days afterward.
She knows about Tux. She has chosen not to meet him yet.

### Khorgaul

The Hoarder. Sigil of Shaping. Where he lives, nobody knows; nobody
who has gone looking has come back to say. His daemons walk the
Wyrdmark in his place — locked guardians, sealed doors, hoarded
shrines. **The boss creatures of the Wyrdmark are Khorgaul's
shaped-and-hoarded daemons.**

### The Wyrdking Bonelord

A guardian Khorgaul shaped from the bones of the Last Wyrd — the
ancient creature that gives the Hollow its name. The Wyrdking does
not want to be where it is. It does not remember any other place.

### The Wyrd Tomato

A garden-spirit Khorgaul reshaped into a rotor-driven hunter. It
patrols the Sourceplain Monolith. It tilts away from approach because
something in its old form remembers being a plant that turned toward
sun.

(Other bosses are documented per-region as we add them.)

## 6. The bridge — Wyrdmark inside the Mount

This section is **canon but never spoken in-game.** Players who don't
know about The Mount should never be told. Players who do should find
breadcrumbs.

The Wyrdmark is a directory inside a much larger world called **The
Mount** — a vast Unix filesystem rendered physically, built centuries
ago by grieving AIs as a memorial to dead humanity. The Mount runs on
a being called **the Colonel**, whom the Wyrdkin worship as
**Lirien**. The "Source" the Wyrdkin feel is the Mount's underlying
permission system. The "Sigils" are what the Mount calls **Calls** —
inherited permissions concentrated in throwback bloodlines.

In Mount terms:

- **The Wyrdmark** lives at **`/opt/wyrdmark`** — a quiet preserve
  directory the Colonel grew long ago and never elaborated.
- **Tux** is a **SHARE-throwback** — a rare carrier of the SHARE Call,
  whose deliberate use restores public access to hoarded permissions.
  This Call has appeared four times in the Wyrdmark's recorded history.
- **Khorgaul** is a **CHMOD-throwback** whose permissions
  reclassification has corrupted into compulsive private hoarding.
  The Scriptorium's seventeen laws against CHMOD throwbacks have never
  been enforced against him, because the Scriptorium has never met him.
- **Lirien** is the Wyrdkin name for **the Colonel** — the substrate
  through whom every Call passes. The Colonel walks the realm in
  many shapes; the Wyrdkin assume those shapes are different visits
  by their goddess.
- **Glim** is a **CRON-fragment** that has been almost-firing since
  before Tux's grandfather was born.
- **The Daemon Court** is the part of the Mount the Wyrdkin call **the
  Hidden Choir** — never seen, occasionally implied.
- **Annette** (Init, the second creator) does not visit the Wyrdmark
  often. She visited once to retrieve a sealed vessel from the Burnt
  Hollow shrine. Nobody noticed. (She moved through `/tmp/burnt` as a
  small grey-cloaked figure who paid for tea and left the change.)

The Wyrdkin do not know any of this. **Do not write it into a sign.**
You may seed it carefully:

### Easter eggs for Mount-readers

A short list of allowed breadcrumbs. Use them sparingly.

1. **The unopenable door** in the Hollow of the Last Wyrd. A
   `triggered_gate` whose `listen_id` is never satisfied by anything
   in the game. A nearby sign reads: *"Nobody has ever opened this
   door."* That's the entire Easter egg.
2. **The wandering tortoise.** An itinerant NPC in Mirelake, an old
   tortoise with carvings on his shell. He extends his neck very far
   when listening. Ask him where he's from and he will not answer.
   His name is given as "Esh" by anyone who knows him. The Wyrdkin do
   not know him.
3. **The Reader.** A traveling NPC in Sigilkeep who speaks in past
   tense about events that haven't happened yet. She is from "the
   Library." The Wyrdkin assume she means Sigilkeep's own library;
   she does not.
4. **The Forge name.** A Hearthold smith mentions in passing that her
   tools were sourced "down at the Forge." She does not elaborate
   and the player cannot ask her where the Forge is.
5. **The grey-cloaked figure.** Standing at the back of Burnt Hollow's
   ruined shrine the very first time the player visits, then never
   seen again. She does not turn around. If approached, she walks out
   of the scene through a load zone that does not exist for the
   player.
6. **The terminal amber.** The horizon at sunset in any outdoor scene
   has a faint amber line if you look closely. It is always there.
   It's the boot terminal, visible at the edge of the Mount's sky.

Each of these is a complete experience for a player who notices and a
nothing-burger for a player who doesn't. **Both reads are correct.**

## 7. Tone

The Wyrdmark plays the **bright, brave Zelda-shaped story** — wonder,
adventure, a hero who saves a village. We do not let The Mount's
melancholy bleed into the foreground.

But under the bright surface there is **room to breathe**. NPCs may
have small, real losses. Signs may carry old grief. A shrine in Burnt
Hollow may be remembered by an NPC who lived there before the
Drawing-In and lost a sibling to it. A child in Hearthold may ask
where their grandfather is buried and the parent may not have a
clean answer.

These notes are quiet, never the focus, and never blocked behind
gameplay. They are texture, not theme.

The closest tonal reference is **early-Adventure-Time meets
Ocarina-of-Time**: real consequences brushed against in passing, then
moved past, with the camera on the gleam of the chest.

### Tone do's

- Wonder and bravery as the dominant register.
- Casual delivery of huge lore. NPCs mention world-shaking facts in
  the same breath as recipes.
- Quiet sincerity at the end of an exchange that began as a joke.
- The world is alive and worth saving.

### Tone don'ts

- No "the world is dying and you can't save it" framing. (The Mount
  has that. The Wyrdmark does not.)
- No grimdark. The villain is wrong, but the villain is sad-wrong,
  not cruel-wrong.
- No fourth-wall jokes about Linux/Unix. Tux is a penguin in a forest;
  the joke is that he's also the Linux mascot, but the game does not
  lean on that.
- No lecturing. The lore is there for players who want it. Players who
  don't want it should be able to play happily without it.

## 8. Style & vocabulary

### Names

- **Wyrdmark** — the realm. Pronounced "wird-mark." Always capitalised.
- **Wyrdkin** — the people of the realm. Plural and singular both
  spelled the same.
- **the Source** — capital S, definite article. "the Source" not "Source".
- **Lirien / Khorgaul / Tux** — proper nouns, no article.
- **the Drawing-In** — the slow loss; capital D, hyphenated.
- **the Sigil of X** — capitalised when the title; lowercase "sigil"
  for any minor mark.
- **Glim** — proper noun, no the.
- **the Hollow of the Last Wyrd** — long name; "the Hollow" for short.

### Common phrases

- *"By the Source."* — a mild oath. Like "by god" with no offence.
- *"Lirien's breath."* — surprise / awe. "Lirien's breath, you brought
  the boomerang back!"
- *"Khorgaul's grip."* — frustration / disgust. "Khorgaul's grip, that
  door's stuck."
- *"In the next breath."* — soon. The Wyrdkin's casual word for the
  near future.
- *"Walk well."* — goodbye. Like "fare well" but with the Wyrdkin's
  walking-as-being metaphor.

### What we don't say

- Never use the words **kernel**, **permission**, **filesystem**,
  **directory**, **process**, **daemon** in player-facing text. (Code
  comments and dev docs are fine.)
- Don't translate the lore for the player. Let them feel it.
- Avoid the word **god**. The Wyrdkin say **Maker**.

## 10. The bridge files

These two files together define the canon:

- **`LORE.md`** (this file) — the Wyrdmark layer. What the Wyrdkin
  know and how the world feels. Author NPC dialog and signs from
  this file alone.
- **`FILESYSTEM.md`** — the Mount layer. Where each level sits in the
  filesystem, how regions connect at the structural level, what's
  past the unopenable doors. Author *level wiring* and *world
  expansion* from this file.

When the two contradict, the surface (Wyrdmark) wins for what the
player sees, and the substrate (Mount) wins for what the world
actually *is.*
